The Chandra X-ray Observatory is one of NASA's four Great Observatories,
alongside Hubble, Spitzer, and Compton. It launched on STS-93 aboard Space
Shuttle Columbia on 23 July 1999 and was boosted into an unusual highly
elliptical 64-hour orbit that carries it from ~14,000 km out to ~134,000 km —
well above Earth's radiation belts and providing long uninterrupted
observations per orbit.

Chandra's high-resolution X-ray mirrors (designed by the Smithsonian
Astrophysical Observatory and built by Hughes Danbury) give it sub-arcsecond
angular resolution — about 25× sharper than previous X-ray observatories.
Its instruments, including the Advanced CCD Imaging Spectrometer (ACIS) and
High Resolution Camera (HRC), have produced landmark images of supernova
remnants, active galactic nuclei, galaxy clusters, and black-hole accretion.

Originally planned for a five-year mission, Chandra passed its 25th
anniversary in 2024. Its thermal insulation has degraded over time, limiting
allowable pointing orientations, but the observatory continues to produce
science despite ongoing budget debates within NASA about funding levels.

Chandra's key scientific contributions include direct imaging of matter
falling into Sgr A* (the Milky Way's central black hole), observations of
the Bullet Cluster that helped constrain dark matter models, and ongoing
monitoring of supernova remnants and neutron stars.
