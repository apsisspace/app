Aqua is a NASA Earth-observation satellite in a sun-synchronous orbit at
approximately 705 km altitude and 98.2° inclination. It launched from
Vandenberg Air Force Base on 4 May 2002 as the second flagship mission of the
Earth Observing System (EOS), following Terra. Its original design life was
six years; it has now been returning data for over two decades.

Aqua carries six instruments — AIRS, AMSU-A, HSB (failed 2003), CERES, MODIS,
and AMSR-E (retired 2011) — that together measure Earth's water cycle,
cloud cover, sea-surface temperatures, aerosols, and radiation budget. Its
MODIS imagery in particular is central to daily global monitoring of
vegetation, wildfires, and ocean chlorophyll.

Aqua flew in the "A-Train" formation with several other afternoon sun-sync
satellites until a 2022 exit manoeuvre placed it in a lower drift orbit
ahead of end-of-mission. NASA declared the mission ended in its prime
Earth-science capacity in 2024 and continues to monitor its passive decay
while selected instruments provide reduced data.

Aqua's long baseline of cross-calibrated observations has been foundational to
climate-trend analyses and weather forecasting worldwide.
