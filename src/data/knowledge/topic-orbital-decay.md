Orbital decay is the gradual loss of altitude a satellite experiences as it
loses orbital energy to drag from Earth's upper atmosphere. Although space
is nominally "vacuum," the atmosphere's exponentially thinning tail extends
far beyond the Kármán line (100 km), and any satellite below roughly 600 km
is in measurable drag. Below ~300 km, decay becomes rapid — a dead satellite
at that altitude may re-enter within weeks to a few months.

The decay rate depends on the satellite's ballistic coefficient
(mass / drag-area), its altitude, and the density of the upper atmosphere at
that altitude. Atmospheric density itself varies by more than an order of
magnitude with solar activity: during solar maximum the upper atmosphere
heats and expands, dramatically increasing drag on LEO spacecraft. SpaceX
famously lost 38 Starlink satellites launched in February 2022 to a
geomagnetic storm that inflated the atmosphere after their deployment.

Active satellites counter decay with propulsive reboosts — the ISS regularly
receives boosts from visiting Progress/Cygnus vehicles or its own Zvezda
thrusters. Once propellant is exhausted or a spacecraft fails, it eventually
re-enters and burns up. Modern debris-mitigation guidelines call for LEO
satellites to deorbit within 25 years (or shorter, per recent FCC rules) of
end-of-mission, either passively through drag or by active deorbit burn.

Most re-entering satellites burn up completely; larger structures (like ISS
modules or rocket upper stages) can survive re-entry partially, which is
why controlled deorbits target uninhabited ocean regions such as the South
Pacific "spacecraft cemetery" near Point Nemo.
