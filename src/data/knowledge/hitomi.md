Hitomi (originally ASTRO-H) was a Japanese X-ray astronomy satellite built by
JAXA with contributions from NASA, ESA, CSA, and several universities. It
launched on 17 February 2016 on an H-IIA rocket from Tanegashima and was
renamed Hitomi ("pupil of the eye") after reaching orbit.

Hitomi carried four co-aligned instruments, including the groundbreaking Soft
X-ray Spectrometer (SXS) — a cryogenically cooled microcalorimeter array
capable of spectral resolution previously unachievable in the X-ray band.
During its brief commissioning, SXS delivered a landmark measurement of gas
motions in the Perseus galaxy cluster, demonstrating the potential of
calorimeter spectroscopy.

On 26 March 2016, a chain of attitude-control failures caused Hitomi to
enter an uncontrolled spin and break apart. JAXA declared the mission lost
on 28 April 2016 after ~38 days in orbit. The debris remained catalogued
under NORAD 41337 as it slowly re-entered over subsequent years.

Hitomi's scientific legacy lives on through the follow-on mission XRISM
(X-Ray Imaging and Spectroscopy Mission), launched September 2023, which
re-flew a calorimeter-based instrument derived from Hitomi's SXS design.
