Low Earth Orbit (LEO) is the region of space below roughly 2,000 km altitude,
though most operational LEO satellites fly between about 300 km and 1,200 km.
Orbital periods in LEO range from ~88 minutes at the low end to ~120 minutes
at the upper bound; the ISS at ~400 km completes a lap every ~92 minutes.

LEO hosts the vast majority of active satellites: Earth-observation platforms,
weather sats, the ISS and Tiangong crewed stations, CubeSats, the Starlink
and OneWeb broadband constellations, and most science missions. It is
preferred because launch to LEO requires far less delta-v than higher orbits,
signal latency is low (~5–20 ms one way), and imaging resolution is much
better than from GEO for equivalent optics.

The drawbacks are short pass durations (a given LEO satellite is over a
single ground station for only minutes at a time), limited coverage footprint
per satellite (requiring large constellations for continuous service), and
non-trivial atmospheric drag below ~600 km — satellites there require either
station-keeping thrusters or will decay within months to a few years.

The lower reaches of LEO are also increasingly crowded, driving conjunction
analyses, debris-avoidance manoeuvres, and international discussion of
sustainable orbital-use practices.
