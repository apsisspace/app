The James Webb Space Telescope (JWST) is a 6.5-metre segmented infrared
observatory operated by NASA in partnership with ESA and CSA. It launched on
an Ariane 5 from Kourou on 25 December 2021 and completed a month-long
cruise, sunshield deployment, and mirror unfurl before arriving at the
Sun–Earth L2 Lagrange point in January 2022.

Webb does not orbit Earth: it orbits L2 in a halo orbit roughly 1.5 million km
sunward-facing-away from Earth. That means its NORAD catalog entry (50463)
exists but is not useful for live SGP4 propagation — standard tracking tools
cannot compute a meaningful altitude or ground track for it. The mission
carries enough propellant for science operations well past 2040 thanks to an
unusually efficient launch insertion.

JWST's four instruments (NIRCam, NIRSpec, MIRI, NIRISS/FGS) probe the earliest
galaxies in the universe, the atmospheres of exoplanets, star-forming regions,
and objects in our own solar system. By 2025 it had already reshaped
understanding of early-universe galaxy formation and provided the first direct
mid-infrared detections of CO₂ in exoplanet atmospheres.

Because it lives at L2, JWST observes nearly the full sky over the course of
a year but cannot be serviced by humans — it is too far away.
