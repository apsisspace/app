GPS IIF-9 (Navstar 72, USA-258) is a U.S. Air Force (now Space Force) GPS
Block IIF navigation satellite launched on 2 August 2014 aboard an Atlas V
from Cape Canaveral. It operates in a medium Earth orbit at roughly 20,200 km
altitude with a 55° inclination in one of six orbital planes — the standard
GPS constellation geometry that guarantees global four-satellite coverage.

Block IIF satellites were the twelfth generation of GPS spacecraft. They
added a new civil L5 signal (for aviation safety-of-life use), improved
atomic clock accuracy via rubidium standards, and offered a longer 12-year
design life than earlier blocks. Twelve IIF vehicles were launched between
2010 and 2016.

Like every GPS satellite, GPS IIF-9 continuously broadcasts precise timing
and ephemeris data. Receivers on the ground combine signals from four or
more visible satellites to compute position, velocity, and time — the
foundation of almost all modern civilian positioning, navigation, and
timing infrastructure. The GPS constellation is complemented by other
GNSS systems: Europe's Galileo, Russia's GLONASS, and China's BeiDou.

GPS IIF-9 remains operational as of 2025, alongside the growing GPS III
series (launched from 2018 onward) that are gradually replacing the older
Block IIR and IIF vehicles.
