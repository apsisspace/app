The International Space Station (ISS) is a large modular space station in low
Earth orbit, operated since 1998 by a partnership between NASA, Roscosmos,
ESA, JAXA, and CSA. The first module, Zarya, launched on 20 November 1998;
the station has been continuously crewed since Expedition 1 arrived on
2 November 2000.

It orbits at roughly 400 km mean altitude with an inclination of ~51.6°,
completing one lap every ~92 minutes and crossing most of the inhabited world
between latitudes ±51.6°. Crews of typically six to seven astronauts rotate
through six-month increments, conducting research in microgravity across
biology, materials science, fundamental physics, and Earth observation.

Propulsive reboosts — from visiting Progress, Cygnus, or Zvezda itself —
counter atmospheric drag and keep the station from decaying. Current NASA/ISS
partner plans have ISS operating through at least 2030, with a planned
controlled deorbit thereafter using a dedicated deorbit vehicle under
development by SpaceX.

As of 2025, the ISS remains the largest crewed object in orbit, massing over
420 tonnes with a habitable volume roughly equivalent to a Boeing 747 interior.
