Tiangong ("Heavenly Palace") is China's third-generation crewed space station,
operated by the China Manned Space Agency (CMSA). The core module Tianhe
launched on 29 April 2021 on a Long March 5B from Wenchang; NORAD catalog
number 48274 tracks this core module, which anchors the rest of the station.

Two laboratory modules — Wentian (July 2022) and Mengtian (October 2022) —
joined Tianhe to complete the baseline T-shaped configuration. Tiangong
orbits at roughly 380–450 km altitude and 41.5° inclination, substantially
lower in latitude than the ISS. The station is normally occupied by
three-person Shenzhou crews rotating on roughly six-month increments.

Tiangong supports microgravity science across biology, fluid physics, fundamental
physics, and life-support research, plus Earth observation using a deployable
co-orbiting telescope module (Xuntian) scheduled to join later in the
decade. A fourth module expansion to a cross-shape is planned.

China has opened Tiangong to limited international collaboration through UN
Office for Outer Space Affairs partnerships, and has signalled that
Tiangong may be the only crewed orbital outpost after ISS retires.
