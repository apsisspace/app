A geostationary orbit (GEO) is a circular, equatorial orbit at an altitude
of approximately 35,786 km (42,164 km radius from Earth's centre) where a
satellite's orbital period exactly matches Earth's rotation — one sidereal
day, 23 h 56 min 4 s. A satellite there appears fixed over a single point
on the equator, which is what makes it the workhorse orbit for telecoms,
television, and geostationary weather satellites.

"Geosynchronous" and "geostationary" are often used interchangeably but they
are not identical. Geosynchronous means any orbit whose period matches Earth's
rotation, including inclined or eccentric ones that trace a figure-8 or loop
over a fixed longitude. Geostationary is the special case where the orbit
is circular and equatorial (0° inclination), so the satellite truly stands
still in the sky.

The GEO ring is a scarce international resource, coordinated by the ITU.
Each active satellite is assigned a nominal longitude slot (typically 2°
apart) and is station-kept with regular burns to counter the gravitational
tugs of the Sun, Moon, and Earth's non-spherical mass distribution.

Drawbacks of GEO include high insertion delta-v (a geostationary transfer
orbit is standard), a round-trip signal delay of ~240 ms, and the fact that
very high latitudes cannot see the satellite at all. These trade-offs are
why low-Earth-orbit constellations (Starlink, OneWeb, Iridium) dominate the
newer telecommunications market.
